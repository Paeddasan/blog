An Experiment on Attention

Author

Peter Raidl

Published

July 7, 2026

Today, I want to share a form of movement practice I regularly engage in. This is not completely separate from a training perspective, but I primarily think of it as a form of practice. Hopefully, it serves as an example that balances the more theoretical, philosophical articles I have posted so far.

The practice I do is based on the idea of willingly switching between internal and external focus during repeated exercises or movements. It is actually quite simple - which does not make it easy.

Short introduction on focus and movement

When we have an external focus during movement, we direct our attention to the information we get from and the effects we have on the external world. It is about where the ball goes, or the cup of tea we want to reach for. It is the more “natural” state during everyday movements. With an internal focus, on the other hand, we bring our attention to our breathing, heartbeat, or the way we use our muscles and joints when reaching for that cup. The internal focus directs our attention to the feedback we get from our body or the effects we have on it (Schmidt et al. 2019, 367).

In research on motor control and learning, internal and external focus have been of interest for decades now. Most research points to a superiority of an external focus when it comes to learning and performance (Chua et al. 2021). On the other hand, there is something to say about an internal focus, too. Many embodied practices (or mind-body practices) like Yoga, traditional martial arts, and some contemporary dance arts use this form of attention 1. Also, the modern mindfulness movement has a strong emphasis on internal processes. Some applied philosophies use internal focus to train right attention or get a better understanding of reality. In a more utilitarian sense, conscious attention on internal processes is thought to improve our understanding of bodily sensations. As emotions probably emerge as interpretations of bodily sensations coupled with environmental cues supporting behavioral changes (Craig 2003; Damasio 2004), this helps us process and be aware of our emotions and feelings.

As practice

But it is not so much the scientific, exoteric promises of the practice. In contrast, I want to raise your curiosity about what happens during such a practice for YOU! To use a word some might find provocative - I see it as a completely esoteric practice. It’s a search into your own internal reality, instead of what is generally known to others 2. So, when I now describe the practice, make sure to understand that the explicit knowledge about what it should be is not of interest. It is about your participatory knowing of what it is to you right now! I want to invite you to try it out - playful but serious. This means you have to try it out more than once while staying open-minded, without waiting for a spectacular effect to arise.

The practice

Before starting the practice, make sure to warm up properly and prepare your space and equipment. Do whatever you need to feel ready.

To start, you need a quiet space and, preferably, some equipment to do a strength-related exercise with. Use one or two exercises you already know. I personally trained with kettlebells a couple of years ago and still use them for this kind of practice. So I do swings and push-ups on some days and alternate between presses and rows on others. Look for a challenging weight that you can move about 8 to 20 times with “good form”. You will do about three to six sets of the exercise without ever going to failure. I typically set a timer that signals the start of each new set so I can focus on my actual practice. Try 60 to 180-second intervals, depending on the relative load and the movement. For starters: the heavier the implement, the longer the rest time. There is no need for more details here. Lastly, make sure that your phone is in airplane mode! This is crucial. You don’t want to get sucked into something else during that time. Once everything is set up, you can start the practice. Every time the signal goes off, start your set. In between sets, walk a bit or just sit down. So far, it might not be so different from your typical training.

The essential thing about this kind of practice is how you direct your attention. When you do your exercise, your focus should be very narrow and specific. Focus on the movement of the implement by envisioning either a target you want to move it towards or something you want to move it away from. For example, while lifting an object from the ground, you can focus on getting it away from the ground as fast and forcefully as possible. Similarly, you could focus on pressing a barbell up towards the ceiling or moving it away from your shirt. Use an external focus and a clear intention that can be expressed in an image (“forceful”, “powerful”, “like a crane”, “like a rocket”…). The moment you finish the set, you flip a switch and bring your attention to your bodily sensations. Notice how long it takes for your muscles to “turn off”, how your blood pressure drops, and how breathing regulates. Stay open to whatever rises to consciousness. There are thousands of regulatory processes going on, and some of them are observable if you take your time. Wait for the next signal to go off and repeat that pattern - That’s it.

Further recommendations

I started with recommending a preparation and warm-up before practice. Nevertheless, this does not mean you have to be in your individual zone of optimal functioning. You don’t need a caffeine boost or be fully rested. It is also interesting to play around with your arousal and your level of fatigue. Just be warmed up sufficiently to feel safe with the exercise you choose.

Keep the practice as simple as possible and try it out multiple times. Maybe you can really embrace the simplicity. It is neither super exciting nor entertaining. If you feel bored during the session, stay with the boredom. It is completely fine. Just flip a switch during the next set and focus on the performance during the lift. Then boredom might arise again or not. Both are fine.

Later, you can play around with the exercise selection by using bodyweight exercises, stretching, or even everyday movements like making coffee. You can play around with more challenging environments, too. For now, you might stick to my initial recommendation.

There is no reason not to turn the whole concept on its head. Switch to an internal focus of attention during the set, and bring your attention to your exteroceptive input (like sight and sounds) during rest. I typically use a broader, more open awareness to contrast with the set’s narrow focus.

A similar idea is Joseph Bartz’s Low Sugar Training. He makes it even more raw. I personally think that some extra instructions help. If you have ever experimented with improvisations, you may know how hard it is to move freely without any guidance or boundaries.

Summary of the practice

  • Find a distraction-free environment.

  • Warm up appropriately.

  • Decide on one or two exercises you want to do. Use exercises you have some experience with, and use moderately heavy weights.

  • Set an interval timer to 60-180 seconds for 3-6 sets.

  • Every time the alarm goes off, you do a set of the exercise without going to failure. Leave 1-3 reps in the tank.

  • During the set, your full attention is focused on an external goal of the movement.

  • You switch to full awareness of internal sensations the moment you put down the implement and stick with it throughout the rest period.

  • Do not add anything to the practice before trying it out at least two to three times.

  • Embrace simplicity and use your curiosity without leaning into any expectations about what the practice will yield.

  • Enjoy!
