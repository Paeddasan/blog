This paper first discusses the concept of stress and its effect on health. We then provide a framework for healthcare professionals to tackle the topic of stress. Lastly, we provide direct strategies to implement in a preventative and rehabilitative context.

**Stress** is an interactive process between the environmental demands, the capacity of the person to handle them and the psychobiological response given. Stress most likely emerges if the situation corresponds to a potential or actual psychological, social, or physical threat to the person.

The described interplay between demands, capacity, and response is hard to measure directly. Therefore, stress is operationalized mainly based on the stress response component of the process. The human stress responses can be categorized into behavioral, cognitive-emotional and physiological effects. The Hypothalamus-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) axis and the sympathetic nervous system (SNS) are core to the stress responses. Frequently described mediators are ACTH and cortisol for the HPA axis, as well as adrenaline, nor-adrenaline, heart rate, heart rate variability, blood pressure and alpha-amylase for the SNS.

Mechanistically, the main driver for adverse health outcomes is chronic dysregulation of cortisol, which affects nearly every cell of the human body. The interaction of cortisol with the immune system creates downstream effects on wound healing, and cardiovascular, metabolic, neurological, and psychological health. 

As health care professionals (HCP) we should think of stress as a potential long-term threat to health comparable to high blood pressure. Chronic stress also impacts health behavior and adherence to treatment plans. Stress interventions should incorporate all levels of the discussed models: The demand, the capacity and the response (behavioral, emotional-cognitive and physiological) and should be executed under the umbrella of client-centered care. Lastly, we discuss strategies that can be implemented in rehabilitative and preventetive settings. 

## What is stress?

The term stress suffered from some nasty inflation in the last decade. Recently, my eight-year-old nephew said it would be stressful to visit us this month and he wants to plan ahead. First, we had a crack over it, but it also made us think about what he is taking from us adults revealing that we are stressed. Stress is generally understood as a state of feeling busy and having a rough time staying ahead of things. The psychologist J.W. Mason (1924 - 2014) described uncontrolability and **uncertainty** as being among five attributes that make a situation stressful. The others are **novelty**, **ambiguity** and **ego-involvement** <mark>SOURCE</mark>. Clearly, no single attribute of the five Mason-facors solely makes a situation stressful.

## What we can do?

Stress is as a frame or concept that is talked about a lot. People use the word to describe their experiance of being overwelmed, out of sync with their needs and desires or struggle to find time time for things that are important to them or are in line with the personality theiy want to be.

Stress can be differentiated based on the nature of the stressor as daily hussels and stressful life events. The daily hussels is what most people use the wording "I am stressed" for. This kind of stress can be described as a stait of mind. Describing stress as being in onse head (or mind) does not mean that it is not real. As Descarde described it, there is just one thing we can be certain of being real and that is consciousness. This is partly represented in his most famous line: "cognito ergo sum". Stress being a stait of mind means that the experiance of stress can not be fully predicted by environmental variables. The feeling we most likely experiance in the stait of stress are one of being out of sync with onse desires and needs. We want to take more time off and relax but we can not find the time to do so. The feeling of stress can also represent itself as helplessness or being overwhelmed, which is often combined with uncontrolability and uncertainty. Again, it is not important if an external observer- you the therapist - thinks about the external validity of that description. It is their reality and there is little chance a short explanation of someone that does not stand in their shoes can change that feeling.

So what is thee better approach?
First we need to make sure people feel heard and cared for. Your listening skills play a crucial part in this game. Nevertheless, I want to briefly point out my personal shortcoming in this regards. Working with clients and patients I was so obsessed with this idea, that sometimes I pushed back even when someone was ready for a solution. Especially for those hcp that are not trained as a psychologiest or psychotherapist, yes listen but remember that you do not have the toolbox to solve the problem this way. 

So let the actions talk for you. Check in with them and see if they are ready, willing and able to create new experiences. You can ask them if they are ready to change some of their behaviour or their daily structure. Yes we have to work with the patient and it is good that we do not order anymore. On the other hand, if they are ready, willing and able to do so, give them clear and concsice instructions and frame it as an experiment. Don't forget that an experiment has to test and therefor measure something.  If the outcome is concerning subjective experience, this is the measurement tool we have to be able to trust.

Every digital kitchen scale starts with the reading of TARE or some other symbole that indicates that it is calibrating accordingly. When you use other measuring devices in your clinical practice you most likely do something similar or you trust in the built in calibration. Similarly our patients (and we) have to calibrate our detectors of feelings and emotions [^1]. You might also think of it as ankering, even if I think this term is not sufficiant. The calibration in this case takes time and is nothing we can push. During this process patients learn to differentiate between different levels of stress and relaxation. So we have to first at least invite both staits. Most likely we can create a frame for relaxation in our practice. Most of the existing literature would then advice to make this experiance explicite; meaning that patient should describe this state most likely via describing bodily sensations. This approach might work for many people. But i am sceptical as this approach relys on the linguistic abilities of the person. Also it takes for granted that language based categorising has some special effect compared to other non-language based categorisations. 

[^1]: I want ot stress that not we are calibrating our patients but they are calibrating their sensory experienace, feelings and emotions.
